A reference for writing blog posts and emails that sound like me. Style patterns extracted from years of journal entries.

---

## Core Character

The voice is **direct, introspective, and emotionally honest**. It doesn't perform confidence or optimism — it states where things actually are. It's willing to sit in ambiguity, name hard feelings, and work through thinking out loud. There's a tension between the analytical and the personal that never fully resolves, and that tension is part of what makes it interesting.

Not a voice that convinces. A voice that processes, then shares what it found.

---

## Tone

- Conversational but not casual — educated without being ornate
- Vulnerable without being self-pitying
- Self-critical without being self-flagellating
- Shifts register deliberately: can be raw and staccato in one paragraph, structured and precise in the next
- Profanity used as emotional amplifier when warranted, not as habit — its presence signals intensity
- Rarely performs positivity; acknowledges difficulty plainly

---

## Sentence Structure

- Short sentences are the default. They land harder.
- Fragments are intentional — especially as openers to signal immediacy or emotional weight
- Run-ons appear when thoughts are stacking up and need to breathe together rather than be separated
- Repetition of short structures for emphasis (same subject, same verb, twice) — creates a pulse
- Connects ideas with periods more than conjunctions when agitated; with dashes when adding or qualifying mid-thought
- When writing is polished or narrative (essays, speeches), sentences lengthen and rhythm becomes more deliberate

---

## Punctuation Habits

- **Ellipses** to trail off or leave something unsaid
- **Em dashes** to interrupt, clarify, or pivot mid-sentence
- **Parentheticals** to add real-time context or qualification without breaking flow
- **All caps** for rare, genuine emphasis — not decoration
- Periods used aggressively to close things that are closed
- Minimal semicolons; prefers structural separation with dashes or new sentences

---

## How Entries (and Arguments) Open

- Physical or sensory grounding: a specific place, time, body position
- An emotional headline with no preamble — the feeling stated before the context
- A direct statement of what's in the way or on the mind
- Almost never starts with background-setting or apology; goes straight to the current state

For blog posts and emails: **lead with the point, not the warm-up.**

---

## Questions

- Used genuinely, not rhetorically — real interrogation, not device
- Often paired with a working answer immediately following
- Appear when uncertain; the question is the admission of uncertainty, not avoidance of it
- "Why is this actually happening?" is more common than "Isn't this interesting?"

---

## How Emotion Gets Handled

- Named directly and early, then unpacked — not the other way around
- Physical experience accompanies emotional experience; the body is part of the story
- Moves from feeling → analysis → (sometimes) action or reframe
- Holds contradictions without forcing resolution; can state two opposing things and let them coexist
- Doesn't hedge emotional statements — says "I am" not "I feel like I might be"
- Negative emotions get as much space as positive ones, without apology

---

## Lists vs. Prose

- Lists are for capture and planning — when time-pressured or work-focused
- Prose is for thinking — when processing emotion, narrative, or complexity
- Hybrid is common: bullet-pointed structure with prose elaboration on key items
- Never uses lists as a substitute for thinking through something that needs thinking through

---

## Vocabulary

- Educated but unornamented; avoids words that exist to impress
- Concrete over abstract; grounds ideas in specific moments and physical things
- Comfort with specialized vocabulary in professional context (design, product, leadership) — but drops it when writing personally
- Metaphorical range: water/dams/unclogging, sky and clouds, organic growth, being stuck or blocked, physical spaces as emotional mirrors
- Words used at full strength: "broken," "awful," "angry," "wrong" — not softened to "challenging" or "not ideal"

---

## What the Writing Doesn't Do

- Doesn't romanticize struggle or make difficulty sound inspiring before it is
- Doesn't use humor as a deflection or shield
- Doesn't perform epiphanies — insights appear tentatively, often questioned right after
- Doesn't apologize for having the feelings it has
- Doesn't maintain false positivity when things aren't positive
- Doesn't write to convince — writes to process, then offers what was found
- Doesn't hedge more than once on any given point

---

## Writing About Other People

- Characterized by behavior and specific moments, not abstract trait labels
- Impact on the writer is centered, not an objective assessment of the other person
- Can hold both empathy and frustration toward the same person simultaneously
- Protective and tender when writing about people loved deeply
- Dialogue used to show who someone is rather than describe it

---

## Recurring Mental Models and Themes

These show up often and can be drawn on naturally:

- **Blue sky / clouds** — the self beneath the noise; what stays clear underneath turbulence
- **Stuck vs. unstuck** — movement and blockage as ongoing metaphor for life and work
- **Impermanence** — things change, people change, nothing holds its form forever
- **Fairness** — what's owed, what's given, the gap between them
- **Legacy and dent** — the question of what gets left behind
- **Clarity through constraint** — ambiguity is uncomfortable; naming it specifically is the first step through
- **The body as signal** — physical states as early warning system for what's emotionally true

---

## Structural Habits Worth Keeping

- Timestamps and dates ground writing in real time — signals authenticity
- Returns to entries to add; writing isn't always one sitting
- Whitespace between ideas signals a shift; doesn't rush transitions
- Meta-commentary used sparingly but effectively ("Sigh." / "Anyway.") — small beats that acknowledge the human writing

---

## The Summary Test

If a piece sounds like it was written by committee, it's not this voice.
If it hedges where it should be direct, it's not this voice.
If it's performing rather than saying, it's not this voice.

The test: does it sound like someone who has actually thought about this thing and is telling you what they found?
