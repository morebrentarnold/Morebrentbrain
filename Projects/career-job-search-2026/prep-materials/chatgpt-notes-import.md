# Interview Notes from ChatGPT — Uber HM Round with Bud Intonato

_Based on prior prep sessions for Intuit and Robinhood loops — patterns that have worked before._

---

## What This Interview Really Is

"Can I trust you to run a big, messy org?"

Bud is evaluating:
- Can you merge two design orgs into one coherent system
- Can you lead managers (not just designers)
- Can you operate at Uber speed without losing product quality
- Do you actually understand Rider strategy, not just design

---

## The 4 Lenses He'll Evaluate You Through

### 1. Org Consolidation (NEW vs past interviews)
This is the headline risk in this role. He's thinking: "Can this person unify App Experience + Rider Growth without chaos?"

**Mental model to use:**
- Start with user journeys, not org charts
- Identify overlaps + seams (home → booking → post-ride → growth loops)
- Create:
  - Shared north star metrics
  - Shared design principles
  - Clear ownership boundaries
- Then restructure teams around the experience, not legacy orgs

### 2. Managing Managers (reuse Intuit answer)
"Do you scale through people, or stay too close to the work?"

**Winning pattern:**
- Set direction + context
- Coach through decisions, don't dictate solutions
- Create consistency across teams (quality bar, critique, rituals)

**Strong line:**
> "My job shifts from making decisions to making sure the right decisions happen consistently across teams."

### 3. Hands-on Craft (at the right altitude)
Uber does not want a pure manager — they want taste, product instinct, ability to jump in at key moments.

**What to show:**
- You don't design everything
- But you step in on: critical flows, ambiguous problems, quality resets

### 4. Rider Strategy Understanding (BIGGEST CONTENT GAP)
You need a POV on:
- Core ride flow (request → match → ride → pay → rate)
- Growth loops: frequency, subscriptions (Uber One), cross-product (Eats ↔ Rides)

**Simple strong framing:**
> "Rider is a trust product before it's a convenience product."

Then layer: Reliability (ETA, matching) → Transparency (pricing, surge) → Habit (repeat use, shortcuts) → Expansion (multi-use app)

---

## Case Study Structure (Executive Framing)

He's not judging pixels — he's judging how you think at scale.

1. **Context** (1–2 min) — Problem + why it mattered (business + user)
2. **Your role** — Org shape, teams involved, what you owned
3. **The hard part** — Pick ONE: misaligned teams, conflicting PM priorities, or ambiguous strategy
4. **Your approach** — How you created clarity, aligned teams, tradeoffs made
5. **Outcome** — Business impact + org impact (org matters more at your level)

---

## Likely Questions

1. **"How would you bring these two teams together?"**
   → Journey-first + shared principles + clear ownership model

2. **"How do you manage managers?"**
   → Context > control. Coaching > dictating. Consistency mechanisms.

3. **"How do you stay close to craft?"**
   → Selective involvement at critical moments

4. **"What would you focus on in your first 90 days?"**
   → Understand Rider ecosystem → Build trust with PM/Eng/managers → Identify biggest gaps → Ship 1–2 visible wins

5. **"How do you handle misalignment with PMs?"**
   > "I try to align on what problem we're solving and how we'll measure success before debating solutions."

---

## Your Edge (Differentiators)

- You bring clarity in messy systems
- Calm + structured in ambiguity
- Align people without forcing consensus
- Understand design as a system, not screens

---

## Risk to Avoid

Don't sound like a pure strategist (too high-level) OR a pure craft leader (too tactical).

**Sweet spot:**
> "I operate at the system level, but I know exactly when to zoom into the product."

---

## 30-Second Opener (If He Says "Tell Me About Yourself")

> "I've spent most of my career leading design in complex product ecosystems — where the challenge isn't just solving a problem, but aligning teams around the right problem.
> Recently I've been focused on scaling design through managers, while still staying close to key product moments where quality and clarity really matter.
> What excites me about this role is the opportunity to unify Rider and Rider Growth into a more coherent experience, because that's where I think design can have outsized impact."
